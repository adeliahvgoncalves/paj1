Travelling to Coimbra

There are a number of different travel options for reaching Coimbra, Portugal. Here we have put together details on how to get to Coimbra by train, plain, bus and car.



If you are wondering how to get to Coimbra by air, it is possible both via Lisbon and via Porto airports. Coimbra is located slightly closer to Porto, however there are more flights into Portela Airport in Lisbon, due to its status as Portugal’s capital city. The onward journey from both is relatively straightforward, with Porto to Coimbra taking around an hour and Lisbon to Coimbra taking around two hours, either by road or train.



Travelling to Coimbra by train is simple. All trains between these two cities stop at Coimbra en route, some on a fast service and some on a slower-stopping service. When travelling to Coimbra by train, whether you travel from Porto to Coimbra or from Lisbon to Coimbra, your journey will bring you into Coimbra B station, which is a short distance from the city centre. However, all long-distance tickets include the five-minute ride to Coimbra A, also known as “Baixa de Coimbra”, or downtown, which is located right in the heart of the city centre. Travelling to Coimbra by bus is a comfortable and cost-effective alternative to the train. You can reach Coimbra by bus from destinations all over Portugal, including the far northern and eastern borders with Spain and from locations on the Algarve in the south.



If you choose to arrive in Coimbra by bus, your journey will end at the bus station in Av. Fernao de Magalhaes, just a 10-minute walk from the city centre and all the major hotels.



Travelling to Coimbra by car is a popular option for those looking to explore the local area. If you are thinking of travelling to Coimbra by car, take the A1 motorway which runs from Lisbon to Porto and choose one of the Coimbra exits which will lead you into the city centre.

The City

Coimbra - History

This city holds a special place in the hearts of all the Portuguese. Rich in its history and is the oldest seat of learning in Portugal with a University founded on the 13th of August in 1290 by King Dinis. This makes it one of the oldest in the world. Coimbra was liberated from Moor occupation in 1064 and was then developed as a town by the capable Conde de Sesmundo who was the master here for 27 years. Six of Portugal’s Kings were born here and between 1139 and 1256 it was the chosen capital of the country. The name of this city during the Roman period was Aeminium, and but it later developed under the influence of the greater nearby town of Conimbriga. This latter place is now an important archaeological site of great interest with a museum to display the findings from the diggings. At first sight the town appears more devoted to commerce than learning but the skyline above the lower part of the city suggest its real purpose.

Environmental Commitment

During the last decade, in which independent music has been the driving force of Primavera Sound, we have experienced the international awakening to a serious, global problem: the climate change.

Being aware of the fact that life in modern society inevitably leaves a carbon footprint, the organizers of the festival are firmly determined to evaluate its environmental impact and to make the decisions and implement the measures to reduce this impact and to offset it.

Some of the initiatives undertaken during the previous editions of the festival have shown us that, by making an effort and putting in resources, it is possible to obtain positive results in the reduction of our environmental impact.

Actions like selective waste collection, using of more sustainable materials and reduction and inventory of green house gas emissions, amongst other measures, will be added to the festival’s organisation and logistics.

And audience, the real protagonist, will have a lot to say in this domain. For this reason we have drawn up a short list of good environmental practices that we encourage you to follow, both during the festival and beyond. Help us to reduce the environmental impact of the festival and to fight against climate change.

1. Come on by foot, by bicycle or using the public transport. It’s 100% effective and easy for the duration of the festival!

2. Reuse your cup if you want to drink the same beverage

3. If you want to drink water use the drinking water fountains. Thus avoiding excessive use of plastic bottles

4. Try to order and consume low packaged and/or locally made products

5. Use the recycling bins to collect separately the waste.

6. Look after the trees and plants: they are our allies in the fight against climate change. Use the special WC!

7. Don’t waste water

We count on you to make Primavera greener!





Environmental Policy 1

Primavera Sound is aware of the potential enviromental impact of its musical event activities, and thus has established and organized an environmental policy in order to: - Watch over the fulfilment of legal requirements, meeting the environmental laws and other aspects considered by the organisation. - Prevent the pollution of the environmental aspects originated by the operation and activities of the festival and study and promote all the available measures to minimize the impact caused by other agents with incidence in the music festival. The environmental policy is developed through technical monitoring before, during and after the event, allowing to establish the key areas, action planning and executing, performance evaluation and operational procedures checking. It's periodically checked to fit into new demands and is communicated to the staff, the suppliers and the audience.

Greenhouse gas offset scheme

During the 2011 edition several aspects were studied in order to calculate the festival direct and indirect greenhouse gas emissions. This inventory estimated some 46 CO2-eq. metric tones. The organization decided to offset these emissions through the carbon voluntary market scheme, which derivates from the Kyoto Protocol, an international volunteer nation treaty signed to fight against the climate change. cerocoThe offset has been made through the ZeroCO2 initiative, carried on by Fundación Ecología y Desarrollo, funding a sustainable forest management project in peruvian Amazonia.
